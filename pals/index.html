Positron Annihilation Spectroscopy

What is positron annihilation spectroscopy?

Using a radioactive sample that beta decays, it is possible to measure properties of a material by detecting how the radiation interacts with the material. Specifically, when the positions are born, they emmint a gamma ray that can be detected. After a while, the positron will anihilate with an electron of the sample measured. Measuring the time between, it is possible to measure the defect density of a damaged sample.

Details About PALS

In a damaged sample, atoms within the sample are displaced from their original lattice positions, with greater displacement resulting in regions of higher defect density. Using a radioactive sample, it will beta decay producing positrons; these positrons annihilate with the electron shell of neighboring atoms, typically producing two coincident gammas. The energy of these two gammas is determined by the rest mass and momentum of both the incident positron and the atomic electron with which it annihilates. Measuring the lifetime of the positron by detecting their gamma rays it is posible to know the defect density of the sample.

Detection System

In order to detect the lifetime of the positrons we need to set up a speciliced system to detect them. This system detects the gamma rays emmited when the positrons are born and dead. These gamma rays are transformed into voltages and signals. If it is detected a birth signal and then a death signal, the system measures the time between them, and sends that time as a voltage to a data adquisiton system.
